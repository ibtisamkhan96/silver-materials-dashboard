Silver added to the US Critical Minerals List in November 2025 (Federal Register 90 FR 50494). The designation reflects silver's irreplaceable role in photovoltaics, electronics, and defence applications, and the US's 77% net import reliance on foreign supply.

Supply-Demand at a glance

Silver has been in structural deficit for four consecutive years. Mine supply cannot keep pace with record industrial demand driven by solar PV, EVs, and AI infrastructure.

Note on silver mineralogy
Context

More than two-thirds of world silver comes as a byproduct of base-metal mining. Mexico's dominance (23% share) partly reflects its massive lead-zinc-gold-silver polymetallic deposits. Peru holds the world's largest known silver reserves (110,000 t) and ranks third in output. Russia (92,000 t reserves) and Australia (109,000 t reserves) are the sleeper reserve stories: both hold more reserves than Mexico, but produce less, suggesting long mine lives and potential growth headroom.

What silver goes into

Total demand fell 3% to 1,162.5 Moz in 2024, but industrial demand hit a record 680.5 Moz for the fourth year running. Solar photovoltaics now accounts for 29% of all industrial silver demand, up from 11% in 2014.

Why industrial demand keeps breaking records
Three structural forces are pulling in the same direction simultaneously
Solar PV

Each standard solar panel contains around 18g of silver for electrical contacts. Global solar capacity additions are accelerating, making PV the fastest-growing silver application. Silver loadings per panel are falling, but panel volumes are rising faster.

Electric vehicles

EVs use roughly twice the silver of an ICE vehicle, mainly in onboard electronics and ADAS (advanced driver-assistance) systems. Each EV requires around 25-50g of silver. Rising EV penetration is a structural demand tailwind through the decade.

AI infrastructure

Data centres and power grid upgrades use silver in electrical contacts, connectors, and switches. The AI compute build-out is generating an unexpected new demand stream for silver that didn't exist at scale two years ago.

The structural deficit

From 2021 onward, global silver demand has consistently outpaced combined mine output and recycling. The four-year cumulative deficit (2021-2024) reached 678 Moz, equivalent to roughly 10 months of global mine supply.

Four-year deficit total: 678 Moz (21,088 metric tons). This represents about 10 months of 2024 mine production absorbed by demand with no matching supply response. The deficit was forecast to narrow to 117.6 Moz in 2025, but structural demand drivers (solar, EVs, AI data centres) are not slowing down.

What 25,300 tonnes looks like

Annual world silver mine production (25,300 metric tons in 2024) is tiny relative to base metals, but it underpins technologies that run the modern world. Here is what that tonnage means in physical terms.

96%
of one Olympic swimming pool

Melted down and poured into a 50m x 25m x 2m Olympic pool, the entire world's 2024 silver mine output would fill 96% of it. Silver's density (10.49 g/cm3) means 25,300 tonnes occupies only ~2,413 cubic metres. A reminder that this is the metal behind every solar panel, smartphone screen, and EV circuit board on the planet.

84 billion
smartphones

At 0.3g of silver per device, annual mine output could supply the silver for 84 billion smartphones. Earth's entire population uses about 6.8 billion active smartphones today.

1.4 billion
solar panels

A standard 60-cell panel uses around 18g of silver for electrical contacts. Annual mine output could supply the silver for roughly 1.4 billion panels, more than double global annual installations in 2024.

0.80 kg/s
mined every second

The world extracts roughly 0.8 kilograms of silver every second of every day. That is about the weight of a standard 750ml bottle of water, ticked off every single second, around the clock, all year long.

1 : 4,000
silver vs steel (by mass)

Humanity produces roughly 1.9 billion tonnes of steel per year. Silver output is 4,000 times smaller by mass. Yet silver commands a price roughly 1,000 times higher per kilogram than structural steel.
